Open Vector

A frontier multimodal reliability lab solving VLM hallucination.

Vision-language models are beginning to read diagrams, inspect claims, guide robots, and reason over video. But they still confidently invent objects, counts, locations, attributes, text, and causal stories that are not actually present in the pixels. That gap is the hallucination problem: language fluency outrunning visual evidence.

OpenVector exists to close that gap. We are building a verification layer for multimodal AI that forces every answer to carry grounded evidence: the regions seen, the uncertainty measured, the alternative interpretations considered, and the point where the model should say, "I don't know."

01

Separate seeing from saying. Our systems first extract a compact evidence graph from images and video, then let language reason only over observations the model can point back to.

02

Treat abstention as a product feature. When the visual signal is weak, occluded, ambiguous, or outside distribution, OpenVector returns calibrated uncertainty instead of polished fiction.

03

Test against the hard cases. We generate adversarial visual tasks: near-duplicate objects, misleading captions, small text, rotated diagrams, temporal swaps, and scenes where the right answer is no answer.

Our research combines dense retrieval, object-level attribution, counterfactual image tests, synthetic data curricula, and human audit loops. The goal is not merely to reduce hallucinations on a benchmark; it is to make VLM behavior inspectable enough for high-stakes work in robotics, healthcare operations, manufacturing, insurance, defense, scientific discovery, and enterprise automation.

We are not chasing a chatbot demo. We are building the boring, exacting substrate that lets multimodal models earn trust: cite the evidence, refuse unsupported claims, and improve when reality disagrees.

OpenVector is currently working with a small group of design partners who have visual data where mistakes are expensive. If your system needs to know the difference between what a model can describe and what an image actually proves, we would like to talk.
